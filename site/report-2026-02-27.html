The Inner Workings of Thought: Today's Deep Dives into Reasoning, Latent Spaces, and Mental Models

Today’s arXiv drop reveals a fascinating tension at the heart of modern AI systems: what we think models are doing versus what they’re actually doing beneath the surface. Across dozens of papers, researchers are peeling back layers of abstraction to understand how reasoning emerges, where it breaks down, and what strange geometries govern our digital thinkers.

When Thinking Happens Elsewhere

The most striking revelation comes from work challenging our assumptions about where reasoning actually occurs. The paper Causality ≠ Invariance: Function and Concept Vectors in LLMs makes a crucial distinction that reframes how we interpret model internals. Researchers discovered that the representations that causally drive in-context learning—Function Vectors (FVs)—are almost completely distinct from the representations that stably encode relational concepts—Concept Vectors (CVs).

Think of it this way: when you ask a model to complete “hot is to cold as fast is to ___”, there are two separate systems at play. One system—the CVs—encodes the abstract concept of “opposites” in a format-invariant way. Another system—the FVs—actually executes the task, but its activation patterns are specific to how you phrased the question (multiple-choice versus open-ended). These FVs and CVs live in similar layers but are largely disjoint sets of attention heads.

This separation explains why models can know something abstractly yet fail to apply it consistently across different formats. It’s like having a brilliant mathematician who can prove theorems in their head but stumbles when asked to explain them on a blackboard versus in a multiple-choice test. The knowledge exists, but the execution machinery is brittle.

This theme of hidden separation recurs in multimodal systems too. Where Vision Becomes Text: Locating the OCR Routing Bottleneck in Vision-Language Models reveals that text-processing pathways in VLMs are surprisingly modular—and sometimes even competitive. When researchers surgically removed the “OCR subspace” (directions in the activation space correlated with text processing) in some models, they found that non-text tasks like object counting actually improved. The text-reading circuitry wasn’t just separate—it was actively interfering with other visual functions. This modularity suggests we could build more efficient systems by disentangling these competing pathways.

The Geometry of Meaning

If representations are separating into functional modules, what governs their overall organization? Symmetry in language statistics shapes the geometry of model representations provides a beautiful mathematical answer: the symmetrical patterns in how words co-occur directly determine the shapes that embeddings form.

The paper shows analytically that translation symmetry—where the co-occurrence probability between “January” and “February” is similar to that between “February” and “March”—forces embeddings to arrange themselves on specific manifolds. Months form circles; years create rippled lines. These aren’t architectural accidents but mathematical necessities emerging from statistical regularities in the data.

This geometric perspective helps explain why certain patterns persist across models: they’re baked into the statistics of language itself. When researchers perturbed co-occurrence statistics (like deleting sentences where two months appear together), the geometry remained robust—protected by the collective coupling of many words to underlying latent variables (like seasonal concepts).

When Reasoning Goes Awry

Several papers today probe the boundaries of what models can and cannot reason about. Large Language Models are Algorithmically Blind delivers a particularly sobering finding: despite their vast knowledge about algorithms, frontier LLMs cannot reliably predict how those algorithms will perform. Researchers tested whether models could predict performance ranges (precision, recall) for causal discovery algorithms across different datasets. The results? Model-predicted ranges were far wider than true confidence intervals yet still failed to contain the actual means in most cases—performing worse than random guessing.

This “algorithmic blindness” reveals a fundamental gap between declarative knowledge (“I know what this algorithm does”) and procedural understanding (“I know how it will behave under these conditions”). For practitioners hoping to use LLMs to guide algorithm selection, this is a critical limitation.

Similarly, GPT-4o Lacks Core Features of Theory of Mind challenges optimistic assessments of social reasoning capabilities. While GPT-4o can approximate human judgments on simple Theory of Mind tasks, it fails at logically equivalent tasks requiring consistent causal models of mental states. The model’s social proficiency appears to be driven by surface-level patterns rather than genuine understanding of minds—a crucial distinction as we deploy these systems in social contexts.

New Frameworks for Old Problems

Amid these limitations, researchers are building clever new frameworks to push capabilities forward. Supervised Reinforcement Learning: From Expert Trajectories to Step-wise Reasoning offers a middle path between imitation learning and reinforcement learning for reasoning tasks. By training models to generate internal reasoning before each action and providing step-wise rewards based on similarity to expert trajectories—even when the overall solution is wrong—the method enables small models to solve problems that neither SFT nor RLVR alone could handle.

For those working with multimodal documents, Index Light, Reason Deep: Deferred Visual Ingestion for Visual-Dense Document Question Answering provides a paradigm shift. Instead of using VLMs during indexing to generate page descriptions (which loses visual details), the framework defers VLM usage until inference. It first performs lightweight, text-only retrieval using document structure, then sends only candidate pages to the VLM for targeted analysis. The results are staggering: +41.3 percentage point improvements on bridge engineering documents where traditional embedding retrieval fails because all technical drawings look semantically similar.

The Practical Revolution

Perhaps most exciting are papers bringing these insights to real-world systems. Towards Faithful Industrial RAG: A Reinforced Co-adaptation Framework for Advertising QA demonstrates what happens when academic RAG meets production constraints. By jointly optimizing retrieval and generation with evidence-constrained reinforcement learning—and introducing pragmatic safeguards like URL validity checks that verify both evidence grounding and HTTP status codes—the team achieved a 72% reduction in hallucination rate and a 92.7% reduction in URL hallucination in production A/B tests.

Similarly, Reinforcing Real-world Service Agents: Balancing Utility and Cost in Task-oriented Dialogue tackles the critical business problem of deploying helpful AI agents without burning budget on excessive vouchers or perks. Their Cost-aware Multi-turn Policy Optimization uses a PID-Lagrangian controller to dynamically adjust cost constraints, converging precisely at boundary targets (like 30.8% voucher rate vs. a <30% goal) while maximizing user satisfaction. This isn’t just academic—it’s deployed and serving millions of interactions.

The Takeaway

Today’s papers collectively paint a picture of a field maturing from benchmark-chasing to mechanism-understanding. We’re moving beyond asking “does it work?” to “how does it work—and where will it fail?” The separation of concept storage from task execution, the mathematical determination of representation geometry, the practical limitations in algorithmic reasoning—these insights matter because they tell us what we’re actually building.

As Cognitive Models and AI Algorithms Provide Templates for Designing Language Agents argues, we don’t need to reinvent cognitive wheels. Established models from cognitive science and classical AI can serve as templates for structuring LLM-based agents. The most exciting research is now at this intersection: combining deep understanding of mechanisms with practical engineering to build systems that aren’t just intelligent, but robust, interpretable, and trustworthy.

The throughline across all these works is a growing sophistication in how we probe, measure, and improve AI systems. We’re learning that reasoning isn’t a monolithic capability but a collection of separable processes—some brittle, some robust, some interfering with each other. Understanding these distinctions will be key to building the next generation of AI that doesn’t just perform well on benchmarks but reasons reliably in the messy, unpredictable real world.
